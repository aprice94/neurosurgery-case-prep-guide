## Surgical Planning

### Diagnosis & Indication
- Indication: Refractory intracranial hypertension (ICP > 22 sustained despite tiered medical management) ± mass lesion needing evacuation
- Goals: Reduce ICP, prevent herniation death
- **Unilateral hemicraniectomy** (lateralized pathology/swelling) vs **bifrontal** (diffuse bilateral edema)

### Position
- Hemicraniectomy: supine, head turned, shoulder roll
- Bifrontal: supine, neutral

### Key Surgical Steps — Detailed
1. **Incision** — large **reverse question-mark** (frontotemporoparietal) for hemicraniectomy starting at the zygoma/root, curving posteriorly around to the midline; **bicoronal** for bifrontal. Reflect the scalp/temporalis as a myocutaneous flap, protecting the superficial temporal artery base when feasible
2. **Burr holes** (keyhole, temporal, posterior, parasagittal) and turn the **bone flap with a craniotome** — **the flap must be large (≥ 12 cm, ideally 14-15 cm AP)** to avoid cortical/venous strangulation at the bone edges and herniation through an inadequate window
3. **Hemicraniectomy:** frontotemporoparietal; **rongeur the temporal squama down to the floor of the middle fossa** (temporal decompression is critical to relieve uncal/brainstem compression); keep the medial edge ~2.5 cm from the midline to protect the **superior sagittal sinus** and parasagittal bridging veins
4. **Bifrontal:** bilateral frontal craniotomy to the floor of the anterior fossa; if needed, ligate and divide the anterior superior sagittal sinus and falx (anterior third only)
5. **Evacuate any mass lesion** (subdural/epidural hematoma, contusion) once dura is opened
6. **Dural opening** — open the dura widely in a stellate/C-shaped fashion to allow the brain to expand
7. **Expansile duraplasty** — sew in a dural substitute (pericranium/synthetic) **loosely** to augment the intradural volume; **never close the dura tightly** (defeats the decompression)
8. **Do NOT resect viable brain**; debride only frankly necrotic/non-viable herniating tissue if it prevents closure
9. **Store the bone flap** — subcutaneous abdominal pocket or bone bank/freezer (label for future cranioplasty)
10. **ICP monitor / EVD** as indicated, **subgaleal drain**, and scalp closure (galea + skin); ensure the scalp closes loosely over the swollen brain without tension
11. Confirm hemostasis throughout (coagulopathy common in TBI — correct concurrently)

### Critical Anatomy & Structures at Risk
1. **Superior sagittal sinus** (midline margin; bifrontal anterior SSS ligation)
2. **Bridging/cortical veins** (bone edge strangulation if flap too small)
3. **Temporal lobe/middle fossa floor** (must decompress)

### Equipment
- [ ] Craniotome, drill, dural substitute (large duraplasty), bone storage, ICP monitor/EVD, hemostatic agents

### Anesthesia
- [ ] Arterial line, central line, mannitol/hypertonic saline, correct coagulopathy, blood available, normothermia/normoglycemia

### Potential Complications
1. Inadequate decompression (small flap)
2. Hemorrhagic contusion expansion
3. Hydrocephalus, subdural hygroma
4. Sinking skin flap / syndrome of trephined (pre-cranioplasty)
5. Infection; herniation through defect if too small

## Surgical Planning

### Implant Selection
- **Autologous bone flap** (stored frozen or in abdominal pocket): cheapest, biocompatible; higher resorption (esp. young/fragmented flaps) and infection-related failure
- **Custom synthetic (PEEK, titanium, porous polyethylene):** patient-specific, best cosmesis for large/complex defects, no resorption; costlier; needs preop CT and fabrication time
- **PMMA (bone cement):** molded intraoperatively, inexpensive
- Avoid autologous bone if prior infection

### Position
- Per defect location; Mayfield/horseshoe; original incision reused

### Key Surgical Steps
1. Reopen original incision; **careful subgaleal/subperiosteal dissection** off the dura (dura often adherent, thin — avoid durotomy and cortical injury)
2. Define bony edges circumferentially
3. Hemostasis (scalp/dura — epidural bleeding common)
4. Place implant (autologous flap or custom/PMMA), confirm fit/contour/symmetry
5. **Fixation** — titanium plates/screws around the perimeter
6. **Central dural tack-up suture** through implant (reduce epidural dead space/hematoma)
7. Drain (subgaleal), meticulous hemostasis
8. Closure in layers

### Critical Anatomy & Structures at Risk
1. **Underlying dura/brain** — adherent; durotomy/cortical injury during dissection
2. Cortical veins, superior sagittal sinus (near midline defects)
3. Cosmesis/contour, temporalis muscle (temporal defects)

### Equipment
- [ ] Implant (autologous flap / custom PEEK-titanium / PMMA)
- [ ] Cranial fixation (plates/screws), drill
- [ ] Subgaleal drain, hemostatic agents

### Anesthesia
- [ ] Cefazolin (± vancomycin per protocol), standard general anesthesia

### Potential Complications
1. **Infection** (higher than typical clean cranial cases; ~5-15%, esp. autologous) — may require implant removal
2. **Epidural/subgaleal hematoma** — central tack-up, drain, hemostasis
3. Bone flap resorption (autologous)
4. Seizures, CSF leak (durotomy), poor cosmesis, implant exposure/failure
